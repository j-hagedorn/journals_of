Looking at the way that the Self, Nature and the Sublime are framed in Romantic-era science, it is easy to see how (even in fields supposedly untinged by subjectivity), the medium conveys and marinades the message.

The converse is also true, and some media are inimical to conveying certain types of messages or subjects. For instance, syllogisms <> mysticism, or koans <> empirical experiments, or tweets <> dramatic monologues. This is not to say that one couldn’t rassle these subjects into these forms; but the result would smack more of the incongruity, or the pomo dilution of meaning itself.

I can’t help but think (or hope) that much of the subsequent erasure of the Sublime from scientific discourse after the Romantic period was due to a greater mechanization of method rather than any failure to experimentally verify the Sublime.1

  1. In some ways, the humility woven into the scientific method itself might be said to have a kind of sublime ratio of self/other at its base.↩
